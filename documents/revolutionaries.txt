Vinayak Damodar Savarkar (1883–1966)
• Roles: Revolutionary organizer (Abhinav Bharat), writer of “The Indian War of Independence (1857),” later president of the Hindu Mahasabha.
• London years: Active at India House; promoted armed resistance and writings that inspired students.
• Arrest & imprisonment: Linked to revolutionary networks and the Nashik conspiracy; sentenced to two life terms and transported to the Cellular Jail (Andamans) in 1911; released on conditions in 1924.
• Ideas: “Hindutva: Who is a Hindu?” (1923) outlined a cultural-political definition of nationhood; advocated militarization and self-reliance; controversial for mercy petitions and later political positions (e.g., criticism of the Quit India movement).
• Legacy: Polarizing figure—seen by supporters as a strategic nationalist and by critics as a progenitor of exclusivist politics.

Keywords: Savarkar, Abhinav Bharat, India House, Cellular Jail, Andaman, Hindutva (1923), Hindu Mahasabha, Nashik conspiracy, Indian War of Independence 1857.

Madan Lal Dhingra (1883–1909)
• Background: Student from Amritsar studying in London; influenced by Indian nationalist circles around India House.
• Assassination of Curzon Wyllie: On 1 July 1909, Dhingra shot Sir William Hutt Curzon Wyllie at the Imperial Institute in London; a bystander, Dr. Cawas Lalcaca, also died.
• Trial and execution: Dhingra was tried swiftly, refused to plead for mercy, and was hanged at Pentonville Prison on 17 August 1909.
• Impact: Shocked British and Indian audiences; became a martyr for some revolutionaries; increased surveillance and crackdown on India House networks.

Keywords: Madan Lal Dhingra, Curzon Wyllie, Imperial Institute, Pentonville Prison, India House, 1909.

Subhas Chandra Bose “Netaji” (1897–1945*)
• Congress to Forward Bloc: Congress president (1938–39); resigned after clashes over strategy; founded the Forward Bloc in 1939.
• Great Escape & Axis route: Escaped British surveillance in 1941, reached Germany via Kabul and the Soviet sphere; later transferred by submarine to East Asia.
• Indian National Army (INA): Took leadership of the INA and the Provisional Government of Azad Hind (1943); sought independence with Japanese support; fought in the Burma–Imphal–Kohima theatre (1944).
• Vision: “Give me blood, and I will give you freedom”; emphasized total mobilization, women’s regiment (Rani of Jhansi Regiment), and “Jai Hind” as a rallying cry.
• Death and debates: Officially reported to have died in a plane crash at Taipei on 18 Aug 1945; later inquiries and public debate left a legacy of controversy around the circumstances.

Keywords: Subhas Chandra Bose, Netaji, INA, Azad Hind, Forward Bloc, Imphal, Kohima, Rani of Jhansi Regiment, 1943–1945.

Connections and contrasts (one-liners useful for retrieval)
• London network: Savarkar’s India House influenced students like Dhingra.
• Strategies: Savarkar favored revolutionary preparation and later electoral politics via the Mahasabha; Bose pursued a wartime army and international alliances.
• Memory: Dhingra as early martyr; Savarkar as polarizing ideologue-organizer; Bose as mass-appeal commander of the INA.
